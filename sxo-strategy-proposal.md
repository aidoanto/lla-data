### What is SXO?

SXO (Search Experience Optimization) expands on SEO (Search Engine Optimisation) by focusing not just on getting people to your site, but on what happens once they're there. It prioritizes the entire user journey, including content relevance and site usability.

While SEO is largely about visibility and rankings, SXO treats search as one part of a broader experience that keeps users engaged and guides them toward meaningful outcomes.

</aside>

### Our approach to search experience optimisation

For many organisations, the goal of search optimimsation is straightforward: make the numbers go up. More traffic, more impressions, more clicks, more engagement. Success is measured by growth, and resources are focused on optimising experiences to achieve that growth.

At Lifeline, our challenge is different. Our SXO strategy must be guided first and foremost by our mission to prevent suicide and support Australians in crisis. This requires us to ask more complex questions and measure success in a more meaningful way.

### Measuring with purpose, not just volume

An increase in traffic to a page like 'I'm feeling suicidal' must not automatically be regarded as a  success. We need to ask what that increase represents.

Is it a sign that we are meeting an increased need in the community? If so, we need to consider the impact on our services and ensure they have the capacity to meet that demand. Our digital presence doesn't exist in a vacuum; it is the front door to our life-saving services.

### Prioritising user need over pure reach

We must try to understand the 'why' behind changes in traffic. Is a spike in visitors the result of our work meeting the genuine needs of users who will have a better, safer experience on our site?

### Our role in the broader support ecosystem

Lifeline exists as part of a wider network of mental health and community services. Our strategy must consider our place within this ecosystem.

What is the real value in optimising our content to capture search traffic that might otherwise go to different mental health service that may serve the user’s needs better? There may be important reasons for this to happen, but these are still questions we should ask.

### Balancing goals with our user-centred mission

Finally, we must maintain a clear internal focus. Lifeline's website serves many business functions including marketing, fundraising, government relations and volunteer recruitment.

Our SXO strategy must ensure that work to optimise these pages never comes at the expense of our core mission: providing clear, accessible pathways to support for our end-users. The journey of a help-seeker must come first.

To apply our mission-led principles effectively, we must recognise that different sections of our website serve different audiences with different needs. A single set of SXO goals and metrics would fail to capture this complexity.

## Category based strategies

### 1. Self led support content

This content is for individuals seeking information and strategies to manage their wellbeing. Their journey is one of learning and self-help.

**Our goal**

To be a trusted, clear, and reassuring source of information. Success is not measured by traffic volume, but by engagement. 

We want to see signs that the user found the content helpful: spending time on the page, exploring related resources, or leaving with a clearer understanding. Our optimisation will focus on answering specific user questions with empathy and authority.

**Pages:**

- `/get-help/support-toolkit*`
- `/get-help/national-services*`
- `/get-help/hear-from-others*`

### 2. Crisis support services

These pages serve people in immediate or near-immediate need. Their journey must be fast, frictionless, and lead directly to help.

**Our goal**

To make the path to our services as clear and immediate as possible. SXO here is about discoverability and speed. 

We will ensure these pages are highly visible for urgent search terms and that, once a user lands, the contact information for phone, text, and chat services is instantly accessible. Every click we can remove from their journey is a success.

- `/chat`
- `/131114`
- `/text`
- `/get-help/im-thinking-about-suicide`
- `/get-help/i-want-to-talk-to-someone`
- `/get-help/i-want-to-explore-my-options`
- `/get-help/services/crisis-support`

### 3. Volunteering

Here we are speaking to community-minded individuals who want to offer their time and skills. Their journey is one of consideration and commitment.

**Our goal**

To attract and inform potential volunteers who align with Lifeline’s values. We will optimise these pages to be found by people actively looking for meaningful volunteer roles. 

The content will be designed to clearly explain the requirements, training, and impact of volunteering, leading to high-quality applications.

**Pages:**

- `/get-involved/volunteer*`

### 4. Marketing and fundraising

This section addresses our supporters, donors, and partners. Their journey is motivated by a desire to contribute to our mission financially.

**Our goal**

To inspire and enable financial support for Lifeline's services. This is where our SXO approach can more closely resemble traditional models. We will focus on making our campaigns and donation portals easy to find and use. 

**Pages:**

- `/get-involved/fundraising*`
- `/get-involved/donate*`
- `/about/our-partners/our-corporate-partners`
- `/get-involved/partner-with-us`

### 5. About us

This content is for a broad audience, including media, potential partners, and the general public, who want to understand who we are. This journey is one of due diligence and trust-building.

At the moment, this rule also includes content from workplace and community training team, which doesn’t fit neatly into this category, but could get broken into its own category in the future.

**Pages:**

- All pages that don’t fit into rules above.

**Our goal**

To transparently communicate Lifeline’s identity, history, and governance.

## Next steps

Before formulating a strategy, we’ll need to analyse the data we have, research the keyword usage in our space, and generate insights that will help us to take action.

### Volunteering (Priority 5/5)

- Query GSC for queries containing "volunteer," "crisis support," and "charity work" to find high-impression, low-click opportunities. Use GA to track the full application funnel from landing page to submission, segmenting by traffic source.
- Identify which search terms drive completed applications vs. casual browsers. Pinpoint the step in your application process with the highest drop-off rate.
- Create a funnel visualization showing conversion rate from pageview to application submitted, broken down by source/medium. Include a table of top 20 volunteer-related queries with impression, click, and application rate data.

### Self-led support content (Priority 4/5)

- Use SQL to find GSC queries where you rank 8-15 (page two) with significant impressions—these are your quick wins. Analyze GA landing page data for articles with high bounce rate but long time-on-page (confusing navigation vs. helpful content).
- Discover which mental health topics have search demand but thin content on your site. Understand if users who read one article progress to related content or exit.
- Build a scatter plot of all support articles: x-axis = organic traffic, y-axis = average engagement time. Add a filter for topic category. Include a separate table flagging articles ranking on page two.

### Support services

- Detect real-world events that increase help-seeking behavior. Understand which contact method (phone, chat, text) users try to reach first based on page clicks.
- Create a simple alert dashboard showing traffic anomalies for service pages, plus a map visualization of service page views by state. Add a table of the most-clicked elements on each service page.
